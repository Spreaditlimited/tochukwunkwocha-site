Parents do not need to become AI experts to guide children well. They need clear rules, practical examples, and a willingness to learn alongside the child.

Children will encounter AI in school, search, apps, social media, and future workplaces. Avoiding the conversation is not a strategy. The better approach is to help children use AI safely, honestly, and creatively.

This guide gives parents a practical home framework.

## Start with a conversation, not a lecture

Ask your child what they already know about AI.

Questions:

- Have you used ChatGPT or another AI tool?
- What did you use it for?
- What do your friends use it for?
- What does your school allow?
- What do you think AI is good at?
- What do you think AI can get wrong?

This helps you understand their current exposure.

## Create home AI rules

Rules should be clear and simple.

Example rules:

- do not share private information
- do not use AI to cheat
- ask before using a new AI app
- check important answers
- use AI after trying first
- do not upload personal photos without permission
- show an adult if something feels strange

Prompt:

"Create a one-page home AI agreement for a child aged [age]. Keep it simple, practical, and respectful."

## Teach the try-first rule

The try-first rule is one of the most important habits.

Before using AI, the child should:

- read the task
- try to answer
- write what they understand
- identify the stuck point
- ask AI for help with that specific point

Prompt:

"My child is stuck on [topic]. Give a hint and explanation, but do not give the full answer yet. Ask a question to check understanding."

This turns AI into a tutor, not a shortcut.

## Use AI together for learning

Parents can sit with children and use AI for safe learning activities.

Examples:

- explain a topic in simple language
- create practice questions
- brainstorm a story
- plan a school project
- create a revision timetable
- review a presentation outline
- compare two explanations

Prompt:

"Teach this topic to my child using simple examples. After explaining, ask three questions to check understanding: [topic]."

## Encourage project-based use

AI becomes more useful when children create something.

Project ideas:

- simple website idea
- storybook outline
- science project plan
- study guide
- presentation
- poster campaign
- quiz game concept
- family budget tracker idea

Projects help children see AI as a tool for building, not only answering.

## Discuss honesty and school rules

Children need to understand the difference between support and cheating.

Support:

- explain this topic
- quiz me
- review my draft
- help me plan

Cheating:

- write my assignment
- give me answers for a test
- create fake sources
- rewrite this so nobody knows AI helped

Ask the school for its AI policy if it is unclear.

## Teach privacy with examples

Do not only say, "protect your privacy." Explain what that means.

Private information includes:

- full name
- address
- phone number
- school name
- passwords
- family problems
- payment information
- personal photos
- other people's information

Use scenarios:

"If an AI app asks for your school and full name, what should you do?"

## Help children verify answers

AI can be wrong. Parents can teach children to check.

Verification sources include:

- school notes
- textbook
- teacher
- trusted educational websites
- official sources

Prompt:

"What parts of this answer should my child verify before using it for schoolwork?"

## Watch emotional dependence

Some AI tools can feel conversational. Children should understand that AI is not a real friend, teacher, parent, or counsellor.

Encourage children to talk to trusted adults about serious worries. AI should not become the place where a child hides distress.

## Create a weekly AI check-in

A simple weekly check-in can help.

Ask:

- What did you use AI for this week?
- What did it help you learn?
- Did anything feel confusing?
- Did you check the answer?
- Did you create anything with it?
- Did you follow school rules?

This keeps the conversation open.

## FAQ

**Should parents allow children to use AI?**  
Yes, with supervision, rules, and age-appropriate boundaries.

**What should children use AI for first?**  
Explanation, practice questions, revision planning, and creative projects.

**What should parents ban?**  
Cheating, sharing private information, fake sources, and unsupervised use of inappropriate tools.

**Do parents need technical knowledge?**  
No. They need clear rules and regular conversations.

## Build AI into learning time, not secret screen time

AI use should have a purpose. Parents can make it part of supervised learning time rather than hidden device use.

Example routine:

- choose a learning goal
- attempt the task first
- ask AI for explanation or practice
- discuss the answer with a parent
- write the final response independently
- close the tool when the task is done

This keeps AI purposeful.

## Use AI for family learning projects

Families can use AI together for positive projects.

Ideas:

- plan a simple family budget lesson
- create a reading quiz
- design a holiday learning schedule
- build a family website outline
- research a historical figure with verification
- create a science experiment plan
- prepare a presentation

Prompt:

"Suggest a one-hour family learning project using AI for a child aged [age]. Include objective, materials, prompts, activity steps, and reflection questions."

## Coordinate with the school

Parents should ask schools:

- what AI tools are allowed
- whether students must disclose use
- what counts as cheating
- whether AI is used in class
- what privacy rules apply
- how parents can support the policy at home

This prevents mixed messages.

## Model responsible use yourself

Children watch how adults use technology. If a parent uses AI to think clearly, verify facts, and create useful work, the child sees a better example.

Show them:

- how you ask for clarification
- how you check output
- how you reject bad answers
- how you protect private information
- how you use AI for planning rather than laziness

Responsible modelling is more powerful than warnings alone.

## Help children build a prompt habit

Parents can teach a simple prompt habit:

- say the task
- say your level
- ask for explanation
- ask for examples
- ask to be quizzed
- check the answer

Example:

"I am 12. I am learning fractions. Explain equivalent fractions with pictures described in words, then ask me five questions."

This teaches children to ask for learning support rather than shortcuts.

## Create a family-approved prompt list

A family prompt list gives children safe starting points.

Examples:

- "Explain this topic simply."
- "Quiz me one question at a time."
- "Give me a hint, not the answer."
- "Help me plan my project."
- "Review my draft and tell me what is unclear."
- "What should I verify?"

Prompt:

"Create a family-approved AI prompt list for a child aged [age]. Group prompts by study, creativity, revision, and safety."

## Review progress, not only usage

Ask whether AI helped the child understand, create, or practise. If the child only used it to finish quickly, adjust the rules.

The goal is growth in thinking and confidence.

## Keep the tone calm

Children are more likely to be honest about AI use when parents respond calmly. If every conversation becomes punishment, children may hide mistakes. A calm tone makes it easier to correct unsafe use, explain better habits, and keep guidance active.
