Children and teenagers are growing up with AI tools that can answer questions, generate images, write essays, solve problems, and simulate conversation. These tools can support learning, creativity, and confidence. They can also create risks when used without guidance.

AI safety is not about panic. It is about practical rules, supervision, privacy, critical thinking, and age-appropriate use.

This guide is for parents, schools, and guardians who want a balanced approach.

## Understand the main risks

The main AI risks for young people include:

- copying instead of learning
- trusting wrong information
- sharing private data
- exposure to inappropriate content
- overdependence on instant answers
- reduced confidence in their own thinking
- fake images or misinformation
- poor source evaluation
- using AI to hide mistakes

These risks are manageable when adults provide structure.

## Set clear rules at home

Children need specific rules, not vague warnings.

Rules can include:

- do not share full name, address, school, phone number, or private family information
- do not upload photos without permission
- do not use AI to complete homework dishonestly
- ask an adult before using a new AI tool
- verify important answers
- use AI for explanation and practice, not copying
- stop and report if something feels uncomfortable

Prompt for parents:

"Create simple AI use rules for a child aged [age]. Include privacy, homework, screen time, verification, and what to do if something feels wrong."

## Teach privacy early

Children may not understand that what they type into digital tools can matter.

Teach them not to share:

- home address
- phone number
- school name
- passwords
- family financial information
- private photos
- medical details
- personal conflicts
- other people's information

Use examples. Children learn better from scenarios than lectures.

## Supervise by age and maturity

Younger children need closer supervision. Teenagers need guidance, boundaries, and honest discussion.

For younger children:

- use AI together
- keep tasks simple
- choose approved tools
- avoid open-ended unsupervised chat
- focus on learning and creativity

For teenagers:

- discuss academic integrity
- teach verification
- review privacy settings
- talk about dependency
- encourage project-based use
- require disclosure where school rules demand it

## Teach verification as a habit

AI can be wrong. Children should learn to check before trusting.

Teach them to ask:

- Where did this information come from?
- Can I find it in a trusted source?
- Does my teacher or textbook agree?
- Is the answer too confident?
- Does it include facts I should check?

Prompt:

"List the parts of this AI answer that a student should verify before using it."

## Watch for dependency

AI can become a crutch if children use it before thinking.

Use the try-first rule:

- try the task yourself
- write what you know
- ask AI for help with the stuck point
- explain the answer back
- finish in your own words

This protects confidence and learning.

## Use AI for creativity safely

AI can help children brainstorm stories, projects, presentations, and website ideas. That can be positive when guided.

Good prompts:

- "Help me brainstorm science project ideas I can actually build."
- "Ask me questions to improve my story idea."
- "Give me feedback on my presentation outline."
- "Quiz me on this topic."

Bad prompts:

- "Do my homework."
- "Write an essay so my teacher cannot tell."
- "Create a fake source."

## Schools need consistent policy

A child may hear one rule from a parent, another from one teacher, and another from a different teacher. Schools should create clear AI policy and communicate it.

For policy guidance, read [AI Policy for Schools: What Should Be Included?](/blog/ai-policy-for-schools-what-should-be-included/).

## Discuss fake media

Children and teenagers need to know that AI can create realistic fake images, voices, and messages. This matters for bullying, misinformation, scams, and social pressure.

Teach:

- not every image is real
- not every message is trustworthy
- do not share humiliating AI-generated content
- ask an adult if something looks suspicious
- verify before forwarding

## Create healthy AI routines

AI should not replace reading, writing, conversation, outdoor play, practical projects, or human teaching.

A healthy routine includes:

- clear purpose before use
- time boundaries
- adult check-ins
- active learning
- verification
- reflection

Ask after use:

- What did AI help you understand?
- What did you check?
- What did you create yourself?
- What would you do differently next time?

## Practical programs help

Random AI use is weaker than guided learning. Project-based programs can teach children and teenagers to use AI for creating, explaining, building, and checking work.

## FAQ

**Should children use AI?**  
They can, with age-appropriate supervision and clear rules.

**What is the biggest risk?**  
Using AI to replace thinking, plus sharing private information.

**How can parents start?**  
Create simple home rules and use AI together before allowing independent use.

**Should schools teach AI safety?**  
Yes. AI safety is now part of digital literacy.

## Create family and school escalation rules

Children should know when to stop using a tool and ask for help.

Escalate when:

- the AI asks for private information
- the child sees frightening or inappropriate content
- someone uses AI to bully or embarrass another person
- the child is unsure whether something is real
- the tool gives harmful advice
- the child feels emotionally dependent on the tool

Prompt:

"Create simple escalation rules for children using AI. Include examples of when to stop, what to screenshot or save, and which adult to tell."

## Teach healthy scepticism without fear

Children should not be terrified of AI, but they should not trust it blindly.

Use language like:

"AI can be helpful, but it can make mistakes. We check important things."

"AI can give ideas, but you still make choices."

"AI is not a person, even when it sounds friendly."

This balanced language helps children build confidence and caution.

## Review tools before use

Parents and schools should check tools before allowing children to use them.

Questions:

- Is the tool age-appropriate?
- Does it require an account?
- What data does it collect?
- Can chat history be reviewed?
- Are safety settings available?
- Are ads or open communities involved?
- Does the school approve it?

Prompt:

"Create a parent checklist for reviewing whether an AI tool is appropriate for a child aged [age]."

## Keep human relationships central

AI should not become a substitute for teachers, parents, friends, counsellors, or mentors. Children need real people for emotional support, discipline, encouragement, and judgement.

A safe AI culture keeps adults involved.

## Watch for social and emotional risks

AI safety is not only about homework. Children may encounter AI-generated images, fake screenshots, voice clones, or manipulative content online.

Adults should discuss:

- not forwarding embarrassing AI-generated content
- asking whether a shocking image may be fake
- not using AI to impersonate people
- reporting bullying or harassment
- understanding that AI chat is not a replacement for trusted adults

Prompt:

"Create a parent-child conversation guide about AI-generated fake images, cyberbullying, and responsible sharing. Keep it age-appropriate for [age]."

## Create safe creative boundaries

AI creativity can be positive when bounded.

Allowed creative uses may include:

- story ideas
- project brainstorming
- quiz creation
- presentation planning
- website ideas
- learning games

Restricted uses may include:

- realistic images of classmates
- harmful jokes about others
- fake messages
- private family stories
- content that frightens or shames people

Clear boundaries make creative use safer.

## Review rules regularly

AI tools and children's maturity both change. Parents and schools should revisit rules every few months. Ask what tools are being used, what problems have appeared, what rules feel unclear, and what new guidance is needed. Safety should evolve with real use.
